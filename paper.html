ATHE ASYMMETRIC JUMPSTART

The stakes compress to one sentence: a high-wage democracy that wants a sovereign, cost-competitive industrial base has one path that is at once domestic, cheap, and not dependent on a rival — automate it (Part B §1). The usual debate then asks "can the US out-subsidize China?", and the honest answer is no. So refuse the premise. The goal is not to win China's game of scale; it is to change the game with moves China's incumbency makes hard to copy. That is what "asymmetric" means here.

Start from a loss the US already booked. America funded electric vehicles and solar with demand — purchase subsidies, tax credits — while China funded cumulative production, and China captured the experience curve. The causal channel is : the cost descent of Wright's Law (Part B §2, ledger C12) accrues to whoever does the making, not whoever does the buying (ledger C28). The lesson is not "subsidies failed"; it is that the US aimed them at the wrong variable. Every pillar below aims at cumulative production instead.

OPEN-ARGUMENTThe six pillars (ledger C31), ranked by doability, chosen for synergy. (6) Compute-for-equity is the keystone, and it leads because it pays for the rest: the state trades fast-tracked power and permitting — and public compute — for equity or warrants in the firms that take them, and those stakes seed the social wealth fund. It is rendered as one jumpstart-native capitalization path inside the fund's existing menu (Part B §4, ledger C24), not the whole fund — so it strengthens the prescription rather than competing with it. (3) Strategic reserves — and domestic/allied refining — the unavoidable work: stockpile compute, actuators, and magnets to buy down choke-point risk the way a petroleum reserve does, and build domestic and allied rare-earth separation/refining, because rare earths and their refining cannot be designed away (ledger C34). (5) Bootstrap the loop with mature fixed automation before humanoids — start the Wright's-Law loop on proven industrial robots and fixed automation, where the learning compounds today, instead of betting the jumpstart on unsolved humanoids. (4) An advance market commitment to "purchase the first million" units (its own beat below). (2) Weld the first production wave to building power — point the initial output at generation and grid hardware so the build-out feeds its own electricity. This is distinct from Part B §2's lever: §2 reforms the interconnection queue so power can connect; this pillar points the first production wave at building the power itself. (1) Obsolete the input — demoted to a narrow, partial de-risk: rare-earth-free actuation removes only the magnet-in-actuator chokehold, not rare-earth breadth or the binding refining constraint (its own beat below, recaveated against ledger C34). Counter-view: every pillar is non-consensus by construction and individually unproven; picking these winners invites capture and misallocation; the synergy is an argument, not a demonstrated plan; and the orbital/lunar input swing stays post-2030. The case is that together they change the variable China's scale dominates — not that any one is a sure thing.

The centerpiece is the : instead of subsidizing buyers, the government promises to purchase the first million units of a target component at a set price — paying for cumulative production, the variable that moves the cost curve. The precedent is real but narrow: a ~$1.5 billion pneumococcal-vaccine AMC accelerated supply and immunized millions, though AMCs are proven mainly in vaccines and live or die on commitment credibility and design (ledger C29).

FORECASTLabeled estimate (ledger C32): an AMC that buys the first million units would out-perform demand subsidies at building cost-competitive domestic capacity — the EV/solar episode (C28) is the counterfactual: subsidies funded demand and handed China the learning curve, where a production commitment would have funded the curve at home. Signpost (2030): an AMC-funded production commitment beats the demand-subsidy $/unit trajectory for a target component. Counter-view: China's incumbent manufacturing scale may simply underprice the commitment; AMC design and credibility risk are real; and the proven track record is essentially one vaccine — the hook is that the very objection to demand subsidies (China captures the curve) is the argument for paying for production instead.

The highest-leverage and highest-risk pillar is to obsolete the input Part B §2 flagged as the robotics chokepoint — China's rare-earth magnets. Rare-earth-free magnets already exist: iron-nitride designs (e.g. Niron's "Clean Earth Magnet") are shipping samples. What is not yet demonstrated is parity with NdFeB on performance and cost at volume (ledger C30). If it lands, the chokepoint disappears; if it slips, the dependence persists. And even a clean win is a narrow one: it frees the magnet-in-actuator, but rare earths are a broad input class — optics, lasers, phosphors, catalysts, munitions, many with no substitute — and the binding constraint is refining, which China dominates regardless of magnet chemistry (ledger C34). Mining and refining capacity stay unavoidable — which is why pillar (3) above carries the load this one cannot.

FORECASTLabeled estimate (ledger C33): rare-earth-free magnets would reach performance-and-cost parity with NdFeB for robot actuators by ~2030, obsoleting that input — but, like Part B's C19, this is the honestly harder call and is likely only partially met given early-stage scale-up. Signpost (2030): [parity metric — torque density + $/kg vs NdFeB TBD].

One swing is worth naming precisely so it is not oversold: orbital and lunar resources — space-based solar power beamed down, or lunar/asteroid material for magnets and metals — could in principle relieve both the power and the rare-earth constraints at once. The dossier files it as post-2030: a real long-horizon option, not a pillar of the near-term jumpstart, and explicitly not load-bearing for any 2030 claim here.

The wager — does this actually work?

Underneath all six pillars sits one diagnosis worth stating plainly, because it decides whether any of this can work. China's robotics edge is not cheaper labor or smarter engineers — it is iteration speed, a kind of industrial metabolic rate: in the Shenzhen hardware ecosystem a prototype can be built, tested, and revised in days inside a roughly two-hour-radius web of suppliers, so the design-build-test loop simply turns faster (ledger C35). That reframes the gap as velocity — exactly what a production-first jumpstart, aimed at cumulative output rather than demand, is built to attack.

FORECASTThe central wager (ledger C36): the first US-made robots would cost more than China's — conceded up front. The bet is not the unit-one price; it is the slope of the cost-decline curve. On a production-first path, US cumulative-production $/unit would cross below the subsidized-import trajectory by [crossover year TBD]. Bull signpost (measured by ~2030): the US $/unit decline rate exceeds China's over the window. Bear case (equal prominence): the curve stalls into a subsidized cost-island — the EV/solar failure mode (C28) — and the crossover never arrives. The wager is stated, not assumed won.
OPEN-ARGUMENTThe honest odds (ledger C37): this is not a prophecy of terminal decline — the US keeps real cards: deep capital, allied capacity, and the production-first lever it has not yet pulled. But the wager is live, not won. Counter-view (both directions): the bull case underweights China's incumbency and its refining lock-in (the very constraint C34 says cannot be designed away); the bear case underweights how fast a production-first cost curve can bend once cumulative volume actually starts to move. Honest odds — not a foregone conclusion either way.

Each pillar above is a bet, and the wager that they bend the cost curve is the biggest — all labeled as bets, and all leaning on the picture the rest of this dossier builds: where the US actually stands (§1), what physically gates the build and what the loop runs on (§2–§3), how the gains stay broadly held (§4), and what it all does to democracy (§5). Part B is that full picture — the evidence and the arguments the jumpstart, and the wager, stand on.

The bet is the slope, not the starting price — and whether America will actually pull the production-first lever in time. The rest of this dossier is what that bet rests on.

Part B · The full picture — what substantiates the jumpstart The standing, the engine and deployment, the distribution, the prescription, and the governance question — the evidence and arguments Part A rests on.

01THE STANDING — US AND CHINA

Where the two economies actually sit. US nominal GDP runs about $30.5–30.8T against China's $19.2–19.6T in 2025 — roughly 1.5–1.6× larger on nominal terms, though China leads on . And the nominal gap has been widening: China slipped from ~77% of US GDP in 2021 to ~65% by 2026, with its population having peaked in 2022 . These are SOURCED-PENDING anchors (ledger C01–C02): sourced and recorded, but not asserted as established until the Phase 3 citation audit checks each against its primary source.

The race is real, but which scoreboard you read decides who is winning.

OPEN-ARGUMENTStrategic argument (ledger C11): for a high-wage democracy, automated reshoring is the most durable path to sovereign, cost-competitive industrial capacity — US wages won't fall far enough to make manual domestic manufacturing competitive, and offshoring hands the industrial base to a strategic rival, so automating domestic capacity is the only mix that is at once domestic, cheap, and adversary-independent. Counter-view: durable is not the same as only — subsidy-and-tariff reshoring (CHIPS Act / IRA) relocates capacity but behind a permanent cost wall; allied friend-shoring shares it but depends on partners' goodwill; and dominating high-value design and IP while letting commodity manufacturing go is the bet the US has actually run for decades. And the catch: sovereign cheap capacity is necessary but not sufficient — if it is narrowly owned while the displaced workforce has no income, you get cheap output on top of collapsed domestic demand and rising instability. The edge converts into durable national power only if the gains are broadly held.

02THE ENGINE — AI AND ROBOTICS PRODUCTIVITY

How much can AI and robotics actually add? The field is divided on the magnitude, and that division is itself the fact: Acemoglu's puts AI at ~0.5–0.7% TFP over a decade, while McKinsey/Goldman estimates run 1.5–3.4% per year, and a single 2025 preprint reports per-task gains roughly 3× Acemoglu's 27% parameter . The durable fact here is the disagreement itself, not any one estimate: this is ledger C03, flagged CONTESTED-MAGNITUDE, and the dossier records the ~10× dispersion rather than picking a side.

One mechanism, though, is not in dispute. Robots are manufactured units, and manufactured units ride — unit cost falls a roughly constant percentage with every doubling of cumulative production, a regularity validated across 62 technologies and, through the WWII demand shock as a natural experiment, shown to run causally from production experience to cost . The dossier asserts that component plainly (ledger C12): a mass-produced robot gets cheaper along an experience curve like any other manufactured good.

FORECASTLabeled estimate (ledger C08): the system-level extrapolation — factories building factories building better robots, a loop that would drive unit labor cost toward collapse — reaches beyond the experience-curve data, which describes cost-per-unit conditional on production continuing to double but does not itself manufacture the doublings. So the runaway is a forecast, not a fact. Signpost: by 2031, a manufacturing unit-labor-cost + TFP threshold [specific numbers TBD]. Counter-view (the bridge): the loop runs on production, and production runs on demand — Wright's Law is silent on where demand comes from once the displaced workforce can no longer buy the output. Broad income is therefore the demand input that keeps the curve descending, not charity bolted onto the machine; this is why the production half and the distribution half of this dossier are one argument, not two.

But a loop that would compound is not a loop that yet runs. Granting the mechanism, the live question for a ~2030 horizon is whether the US can physically stand the capacity up — and there the binding constraints split cleanly. Compute is gated by domestic power and grid: data-center grid demand runs ~61.8 GW (2025) toward ~134.4 GW (2030), the DOE projects ~100 GW of new peak by 2030 (about half from data centers), ~2,300 GW sits stuck in , and a new connection takes 4–10 years against 2–3-year builds . Robotics is gated not by domestic power but by China's component supply chain: China controls ~88–91% of refined rare earths and ~90% of magnet processing, an Optimus Gen 2 costs roughly 3× more without Chinese suppliers ($46k → $131k bill of materials), and Western mine development averages ~17.8 years — not decouplable by 2030 . Cutting across both is a skilled-trades shortage: electricians are now named the top data-center bottleneck by Nvidia and Microsoft, with ~300k+ needed against ~20k/yr retiring, a construction shortfall of ~340k–499k by end-2026, and electrical work running 45–70% of build cost . These are SOURCED-PENDING anchors (ledger C13–C15).

One finding reorders the usual intuition: capital is not the binding input. Across the build-out the gating constraints are named, repeatedly, as power, then labor, then components — "not capital or land" (ledger C16). That is what drives the dossier's lever ranking:

OPEN-ARGUMENTLever ranking for a ~2030 deployment (ledger C17): rank the levers by leverage, not by familiarity. (1) Bottleneck-removal first — , onsite generation, and demand-flexible siting attack the constraint that actually binds. (2) Federal industrial policy second — a DoD-equity/offtake model, recycling plus allied sourcing plus rare-earth-free magnets, and trades training via vocational Pell. (3) Private capital and market design last, precisely because capital is abundant and not the gate. Counter-view: state-led bottleneck-clearing carries its own risks — misallocation and regulatory capture; markets may in fact clear power faster than FERC reform does; and a ~2030 horizon may simply be infeasible regardless of which lever is pulled first. The ranking is a bet on where leverage sits, not a guarantee it can be applied in time.

Honesty demands a stress-test of the sovereignty argument (C11): on the robotics half, the US is not sovereign today. The rare-earth and magnet dependence above is the soft spot — a supply chain that runs through China at almost every step. That is not a refutation of the reshoring argument; it is the reason it is urgent. A sovereign compute base bolted to an adversary-controlled actuator supply is half a base — and the half that is missing is the physical one.

FORECASTLabeled estimate (ledger C18): the US would clear the power-grid and skilled-trades constraints enough to support meaningful AI+robotics capacity by ~2030 — the achievable horizon, hard but within reach if the bottleneck levers are pulled. Signpost (2030): [grid capacity-add TBD] + [trades-gap-closure TBD].
FORECASTLabeled estimate (ledger C19): domestic and allied rare-earth-magnet supply would cover only [X% TBD] of US robotics-component demand by 2030 — the honestly harder call. Given the ~17.8-year mine-development timelines it would likely be only partially met; the signpost is set to reveal the residual China-dependence, not to flatter it. Signpost (2030): [domestic + allied share of magnet demand TBD].

Clear these constraints and you get capacity — but Wright's Law says capacity only compounds with demand, and where the demand comes from once the work is automated is the distribution question. That is the next section.

03WHO CAPTURES THE GAINS

If the engine delivers, who gets the output — and can the loop even keep running? Section 2's capacity compounds only the way Wright's Law says it does (C12/C08): with continued production growth, and production growth needs demand. That is the rub automation creates. As machines do more of the work, income shifts from labor to capital — and the falls sharply up the income ladder. The top group's MPC out of transitory income is roughly zero — for about half the population, essentially zero — so shifting income upward mechanically removes spending from the economy (ledger C20). That is the underconsumption worry Summers revived from Hansen: a productivity boom can still leave demand short.

How big is that drag? Honestly, contested — and the dossier flags it CONTESTED-MAGNITUDE, the same discipline applied to the productivity numbers in section 2. Auclert and Rognlie find the long-run output effect of inequality small when inequality is modeled as fixed household differences, but around 2% when it runs through income risk and volatility instead; they also note a falling labor share can mitigate stagnation through one channel, and that fiscal, monetary, and other demand components can offset the rest (ledger C21). The underconsumption story is a live hypothesis with real offsetting channels, not a settled law — and the dossier states it that way.

OPEN-ARGUMENTThe analytical turn (ledger C22): here the contested magnitude cuts toward the argument, not against it. By Auclert and Rognlie's own logic the demand drag is largest precisely in the income-risk-and-volatility case — and automation-driven displacement is that case: it is volatility, not a stable gap. So broad income is not a transfer away from the production loop; it is an input to keeping the Wright's-Law loop compounding — the demand that funds the next doubling. Counter-view: Auclert and Rognlie's headline finding is that the general effect is small; other demand channels and fiscal/monetary policy can offset the shortfall; and the automation-specific extension of their volatility result is ours, not theirs — they did not make this claim. It is an argument built on their mechanism, and it is labeled as one.

Read that way, the familiar redistributive tools stop being charity and become plumbing for demand. A (Alaska's Permanent Fund is the working example , ledger C05) paying a routes the rising capital share straight into high-MPC hands; an unconditional cash floor — a like the OpenResearch trial (ledger C04) — keeps a spending base under the economy; and a captures the land-rents capital escapes into. These are the components of the dossier's "plug" argument (ledger C07, detailed in section 4) — here seen from the demand side rather than the fairness side.

That demand-side view answers the obvious objection — why not just print a UBI? Because an inflation- or tax-funded cash transfer leaks: asset-holders dodge it, the new money bids up the very equities and land the wealthy already own, and the gap reopens. The social wealth fund is the structural plug precisely because it changes ownership, not just cash flow — when everyone owns a slice of the appreciating capital stock, the gains are shared at the source instead of chased after the fact; and the land value tax does the same job for land. That is the escape-gap the "plug" is built to close.

OPEN-ARGUMENTWhat crypto is and isn't here (ledger C23): crypto is a delivery rail and a layer for distribution — useful for routing payments and verifying each recipient is a real, single human — but it is not the structural plug for asset-escape; ownership of real productive capital is. Tellingly, the flagship crypto-UBI project, World/Worldcoin, dropped the UBI framing by 2026 (ledger C06). Counter-view: programmable transfers, censorship-resistance, and financial inclusion may carry independent value — the claim is only that crypto is the rail, not the reservoir.

Which of these distribution paths a society actually takes is what decides whether the abundance is broadly held or narrowly captured — and that is the governance question this dossier poses and refuses to resolve (ledger C09 / C10).

04THE RENEWAL PRESCRIPTION

Section 3 left a baton: broad income is the demand input the production loop needs, not a transfer away from it (ledger C22). This section picks it up and asks the engineering question — by what concrete mechanism? The dossier's answer is the bundled "plug" it has carried from the start, and labels as argument, not fact:

OPEN-ARGUMENTPrescription (ledger C07): a social wealth fund + a universal basic dividend + a land value tax, together, as the "plug" for wealth's escape into equities and land. This is a policy argument, not a measured result. Each component is entered with its own strongest counter-view — fund governance and capture risk; dividend inflation pass-through; land-value-tax assessment and transition costs — and the dossier rides none of them as settled.

Start with the fund. In the design Matt Bruenig sets out, every citizen holds one non-transferable share, and the fund pays a universal basic dividend out of its returns — Bruenig sets the dividend at roughly 4–5% of a five-year moving average of fund value. One illustrative calculation he gives: a $10 trillion fund throwing off ~$400 billion a year, or "several thousand dollars per person per year" (ledger C24). Capitalization is gradual and can draw on many streams — corporate share levies, wealth and inheritance taxes, leasing public assets, carbon and financial-transaction taxes, even money creation. For scale, Alaska's Permanent Fund holds ~$60 billion, about 113% of state GDP; a US-equivalent ratio would be on the order of $22.6 trillion — which is exactly why this is a gradual accumulation with a modest near-term dividend, not an overnight windfall. Every figure here is an attributed proposal parameter, not the dossier's own projection.

Now the land value tax. Its appeal is efficiency: because the supply of land is fixed, a tax on its unimproved value creates almost no , the burden stays on the landowner rather than passing through to tenants, and it captures the that otherwise accrues untaxed — a property economists from Adam Smith to Henry George prized, with unusually broad cross-ideological acceptance among economists who know it (ledger C25). But the efficiency case never travels alone. It has three documented holes: assessment — separating land value from the value of improvements on it is genuinely hard; the search-and-discovery distortion — taxing away the reward for discovering a better use of land can discourage that discovery (Caplan-Gochenour); and transition — imposing it on current owners expropriates people who bought at untaxed prices, a Tullock transitional-gains trap that forces a decade-long phase-in (ledger C26).

How much could it raise? A back-of-envelope figure sometimes cited puts the US land base around $30–35 trillion and a broad LVT at on the order of ~14% of the relevant base — but the dossier flags that as exactly that: back-of-envelope and contested, sensitive to assessment, to the effect, and to the phase-in path. It is illustrative, not a projection.

OPEN-ARGUMENTDesign logic of the prescription (ledger C27): the pieces fit a sequence. (a) Fund the floor from the asset base, not currency debasement — the escape-gap logic of section 3: an inflation-funded transfer leaks to asset-holders, an ownership stake does not. (b) As automation raises the capital share of income, the social wealth fund routes that rising share to high-MPC citizen-owners — the direct tie-back to the demand-input argument (C22). (c) Use the land value tax as the land-specific plug, phased in to absorb the transition shock. (d) Because the dividend scales with fund size, this is deliberately gradual — not an overnight UBI. Counter-view: the ~$22.6 trillion US-equivalent fund (the attributed figure above, ledger C24) cannot be accumulated quickly, so near-term dividends are modest; public capital allocation invites politicization and capture; the LVT's assessment, search-distortion, and transition costs are all real; wealth and market-cap levies face asset-mobility and valuation problems; and a federal LVT or wealth tax raises an unresolved US constitutional direct-tax / apportionment question — flagged here as an open question, not a settled holding. The prescription is a coherent design bet, labeled as one, with its load-bearing objections in view.

Whether a mechanism like this is adequate — whether it can actually keep the gains broadly held as the machines scale — is the hinge the governance question turns on: broadly held, or narrowly captured (ledger C09 / C10).

05THE GOVERNANCE QUESTION

Does AI-driven abundance strengthen democracy or dissolve it? This dossier poses that question and deliberately declines to answer it — the honest position is that it is genuinely open, so the two horns are set side by side at equal prominence (ledger C09 / C10), neither adopted as the dossier's own view:

OPEN-UNVERIFIED · CONTESTEDThesis (C09): AI-driven abundance is, on net, democracy-preserving — rising material security widens the franchise's base and defuses zero-sum politics.
OPEN-UNVERIFIED · CONTESTEDCounter-claim (C10), entered at equal prominence: the same abundance enables techno-authoritarian concentration — automation removes labor's bargaining leverage and centralizes the means of production and surveillance. Neither side is asserted as settled; the premise itself is examined and labeled.

Which way it breaks is not ours to declare here — it is the question this dossier exists to keep open and honest, not to close.
